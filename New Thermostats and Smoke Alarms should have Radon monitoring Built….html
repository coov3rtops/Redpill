The EPA action level for radon in residential and school environments is 4 picocuries per liter (pCi/L) (150 becquerels per cubic meter, Bq/m³). 

 If testing reveals radon levels at or above this threshold, the EPA recommends installing a mitigation system to reduce exposure. 

While 4 pCi/L is the standard for taking action, the EPA notes that no level of radon is completely safe.

Why No Integrated Product Yet?

Radon sensors (typically pulsed ionization chambers or alpha spectrometry) add cost, size, and power considerations. They excel in dedicated monitors for accurate long-term/low-level readings. Market demand focuses on separate IAQ devices that integrate via software.

why can't this system exhaust near the ground outdoors?

Thought for 4s
Radon mitigation systems (like the active sub-slab or drain-tile depressurization ones we discussed) should not exhaust near the ground outdoors primarily to prevent re-entrainment—where the discharged radon gas gets pulled back into the home (or neighboring buildings) through windows, doors, vents, foundation cracks, or air intakes.

Why High Exhaust Is Required (EPA, ANSI/AARST, and State Standards)

Standard guidelines (e.g., EPA Radon Mitigation Standards, ASTM E2121, and most state codes) mandate:
  • Discharge point at least 10 feet above ground level.
  • Above the roof eave (often 12+ inches above the roof surface for roof penetrations).
  • 10 feet horizontally from any windows, doors, or openings into conditioned spaces (or 2+ feet above them if closer vertically).
  • 10 feet from openings into adjacent buildings.
  • Directed vertically upward (45° elbow allowed in some cases; no downward or rain caps that block flow).
These rules ensure the radon disperses high in the air, where it dilutes rapidly to near-background levels within a few feet.

Key Reasons Exhausting Near the Ground Is Problematic

  • High radon concentrations in exhaust: The fan pulls soil gas that can contain thousands to tens of thousands of pCi/L of radon—far above safe levels. Near-ground release keeps this concentrated plume close to the house.
  • Re-entry risk: Wind, building pressure differences, or stack effect can carry it back inside via lower openings, siding gaps, or HVAC intakes. Studies show ground-level exhaust can contribute noticeably more indoor radon in some cases compared to roof discharge.
  • Direct exposure: People (especially children playing outside), pets, or passersby could breathe higher concentrations near ground level. Roof discharge pushes it upward and away.
  • Practical issues: Low exhaust can lead to moisture/condensation problems, ice buildup in cold climates (though some northern areas allow limited above-ground-level options with precautions), or aesthetic/odor concerns.
Note on recent research: Some studies (e.g., 2025 re-entrainment investigations) suggest properly placed ground-level or low-wall exhaust might be viable in certain homes with low exhaust concentrations, good dispersion, and testing—offering benefits like easier installs or less ice risk. However, U.S. standards still require high discharge for safety and consistency, as re-entrainment risks aren't zero.

For Your DIY Outdoor Fan Setup (Sump/Drain-Tile)

Route the PVC vent pipe up the exterior wall (or through the roof) to meet the 10-ft+ height and separation rules. Use outdoor-rated materials, secure it well, and add a screen (not a cap) at the top. Include a manometer to monitor suction.
Always test radon levels inside before and after installation (short- and long-term kits). If you're in a cold climate or have specific site constraints, a certified mitigator can advise on code-compliant options.

Indoor radon mitigation systems that utilize the sump/drain tile as the suction/collection point (not as the exhaust itself) are variations of Active Soil Depressurization, specifically Sump/Drain-Tile Depressurization (Sump/DTD or DTD with interior sump).
The drain tile network (perforated pipes around the foundation) serves as an excellent built-in manifold to collect and distribute suction. The system pulls radon-laden soil gas from the tiles/sump and exhausts it outdoors via a separate sealed PVC pipe (never using the drain tile itself as the exhaust path).

Key Indoor-Compatible Configurations

These allow the fan to be mounted indoors (e.g., attic, garage, or utility area) while tying into the sump/drain tile:
  1. Sump Pit Suction (Most Common Indoor Setup)
    • Seal the sump with an airtight lid (e.g., Jackel or custom Lexan).
    • Connect the suction pipe directly to the lid or through the floor into the drain tile loop near the sump.
    • Fan draws from the sealed sump/tiles.
    • Exhaust pipe routes up through the house (band joist, attic, or roof penetration) and discharges high outdoors (10+ ft above ground, away from windows).
    • Fan placement: Often in the attic or garage (indoor-rated or weather-protected). Avoid conditioned living spaces per standards.
    • Best for: Basements with interior sumps tied to drain tiles. Excellent distribution around the perimeter.
  2. Remote Tile Suction (Indoor Fan Option)
    • Core through the floor at a convenient indoor spot away from the sump and tie directly into the drain tile loop.
    • Seal the sump separately (or use it as backup).
    • This can make indoor routing easier for exhaust stack.
    • Common when sump access is tricky or for better pressure field extension.
Important: The exhaust is always a dedicated pipe to the outdoors — not through the drain tile. Drain tiles handle suction and water drainage only. Check valves are used on exterior daylight discharges (if any) to prevent short-circuiting, not inside the sump for water pull.

Recommended Indoor/Attic-Friendly Fans for These Systems

Low-to-moderate suction, higher-flow fans work well with drain tile (porous distribution):
  • RadonAway RP140 / RP145 — Quiet, popular for sump/drain tile; ~$150–$170.
  • Festa Spirit / Maverick — Energy-efficient, good for indoor/attic mounts.
  • Fantech RN1 / RN2 — Reliable for attic installs.
  • Larger options (RP260, etc.) for bigger homes or tighter conditions.
DIY Cost Range: $300–$900 total (fan + pipe + sealed lid + manometer). Professional: $1,000–$2,000.
