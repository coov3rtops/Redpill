Generator Noise at Full Power and No Load
(Note: Every 10 dBA increase represents a perceived doubling of loudness to the human ear).

Combine C Weighting with the ISO 9613-2:2024 standard to more accurately measure low frequency noise.

  • Full Power / Full Load (100%): 105 to 115 dBA. This is equivalent to standing next to a jet engine takeoff, a live rock concert, or a chainsaw. It can cause immediate hearing damage without protection.[1]
  • Running Normally / Idle / No Load (0% to Low Load): 75 to 85 dBA. This is equivalent to heavy city traffic or a loud vacuum cleaner. While technically safer, it is still a loud, highly intrusive industrial environment

Active Noise Cancellation Systems

Active noise cancellation (ANC) systems exist for low-frequency noise in the 20–200 Hz range, but they have significant limitations at high amplitudes like 115 dB.

Current State of Active Noise Cancellation for This Scenario

Aspect Feasibility Details
Frequency Range (20–200 Hz) Excellent ANC works best at low frequencies
Amplitude (115 dB source) Very Difficult Most commercial systems max out at 90–100 dB
Exhaust Stack Application Possible but expensive Used in some industrial settings
Overall Effectiveness Moderate (10–25 dB reduction) Best as a supplement, not primary solution

Available Active Noise Cancellation Options

1. Best Commercial Options for Industrial Low-Frequency Noise
  • Siemens / Müller-BBM Active Silencer Systems
    • Designed for large ducts and exhausts.
    • Can handle up to ~110 dB in some configurations.
    • Typical reduction: 15–25 dB in 20–200 Hz range.
    • Cost: $150,000 – $400,000+ depending on size.
  • Lord Corporation / Parker Hannifin Active Noise Control
    • Used on large fans and exhausts.
    • Good for 30–150 Hz.
    • Reduction: 10–20 dB.
  • Custom University/Research Systems
    • Some companies (e.g., in mining or power generation) have deployed prototype systems that achieve 20–30 dB reduction on high-amplitude low-frequency noise.
2. Practical Limitations at 115 dB
  • Most commercial ANC systems are designed for cabin or room use (70–90 dB range).
  • At 115 dB, the energy is extremely high — speakers would need to output nearly the same power as the source to cancel it, which is physically challenging and expensive.
  • Current best realistic reduction for 115 dB low-frequency exhaust: 15–25 dB (bringing it down to 90–100 dB).

Recommended Combined Approach (Most Realistic)

To get 115 dB down toward 50 dB during inversion:
  1. Primary: High-performance passive muffler (55–70 dB reduction) — most important.
  2. Secondary: Active Noise Cancellation on the exhaust duct (additional 15–25 dB).
  3. Tertiary: Tall stack (80–100+ ft).
Expected Total Reduction: 70–90 dB
Resulting Ground Level: Likely below 50 dB within 1–3 miles even during inversion.

Bottom Line

  • Yes, active noise cancellation exists for 20–200 Hz.
  • No, it cannot realistically take 115 dB all the way to 50 dB by itself.
  • Best used as a supplement to a strong passive muffler + tall stack.

Here's a practical, real-world recommendation for live low-frequency noise monitoring and active mitigation for your generators (assuming 115 dB low-frequency output in the 20–200 Hz range).

1. Number of Live Low-Frequency Noise Monitors Recommended

Site Layout Number of Monitors Placement
Small site (< 5 acres) 3 – 4 Property corners + nearest sensitive receiver
Medium site (5–20 acres) 5 – 8 Grid pattern + property boundary + 1–2 off-site
Large / industrial site 8 – 12+ Full perimeter + key community locations

Recommended Minimum for Most Generator Sites: 6 monitors
Strategic Placement:
  • 2–3 on your property boundary (downwind direction is most important).
  • 2–3 at the nearest residential/commercial properties.
  • 1 at the highest elevation point (for inversion detection).
  • 1 mobile unit that can be moved during complaints.

2. Recommended Distance for Active Live Mitigation System

An active live mitigation system (real-time noise cancelling or load modulation) should be triggered at these distances:
Trigger Distance Purpose Action
0.5 – 1 mile Early warning Alert operators, begin load reduction
1 – 2 miles Primary mitigation zone Activate active cancellation + reduce generator load
2 – 4 miles Community protection Full load reduction + secondary measures (mufflers, stack)

Best Practice Setup:
  • Place primary sensors at 1.5 miles radius (this is the sweet spot for early detection before complaints).
  • Use a central system that automatically:
    • Reduces generator load (e.g., from 100% to 60–70%).
    • Activates active noise cancellation if installed.
    • Sends alerts to operators.
    • Temporarily switches to battery backup systems.

Recommended Full System Architecture

  1. Live Monitors (6–8 total)
    • Class 1 precision low-frequency microphones (20–200 Hz focus).
    • Real-time data logging with 1-second resolution.
    • Weather station integration (for inversion detection).
  2. Active Mitigation Trigger Points
    • Level 1 (Alert): 45 dB at 1 mile → Notify operators.
    • Level 2 (Action): 50 dB at 1.5 miles → Reduce load + activate ANC.
    • Level 3 (Emergency): 55 dB at 2 miles → Major load shed or shutdown.
  3. Active Noise Cancellation / Load Control
    • Tie monitors to generator control system for automatic power reduction.
    • Consider hybrid passive + active silencers on exhaust.
    • Switches over to battery backup system under active Inversion until inversion clears.
    • Multiple units must alert before changes take place to rule out false triggers.
    • Utilize ISEE/OSM mining criteria using State/Local decibel limits.

3rd Party Seismograph and Construction Noise monitoring companies should install and maintain these systems for compliance.  Lower cost meters and devices like Iphone, Android, GoPro or voice recorders with external microphones can be used to fill-in and verify measurements with 3rd party monitoring companies who address neighbor complaints.
