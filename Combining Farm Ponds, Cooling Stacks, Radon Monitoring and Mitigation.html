In Ohio, most irrigated farms rely primarily on groundwater from wells, though a significant portion also use on-farm surface water (rivers, creeks, ponds, etc.), and some use both or off-farm sources.

Key 2023 Data (from USDA NASS 2023 Irrigation and Water Management Survey, following the 2022 Census of Agriculture)

  • Total irrigated farms in Ohio: ~805.
  • Total irrigated acres: ~36,969.
By water source (farms and acres can overlap if multiple sources are used):
  • Groundwater from on-farm wells: 739 farms (~92% of irrigated farms), 28,776 acres (~78% of irrigated acres).
  • On-farm surface water: 433 farms (~54% of irrigated farms), 15,115 acres (~41% of irrigated acres).
Groundwater dominates in volume applied as well (most of the water used comes from wells). Off-farm water suppliers (e.g., districts or other sources) are less common but supplement some operations.

Context and Notes

  • Ohio has relatively limited irrigation overall (dryland/rainfed farming is the norm for most of the state's agriculture), with only a few thousand farms irrigating. Irrigation is supplemental and concentrated in certain crops/areas.
  • Many farms use a mix of sources depending on availability, drought conditions, and infrastructure. Wells are reliable and common due to aquifers, while surface water (ponds, streams) requires filtration and can be less consistent.
  • Data are from the most recent detailed USDA survey (2023). Earlier censuses (e.g., 2017/2018) show similar patterns, with groundwater as the leading source.
For the latest or more granular county-level details, check the full USDA NASS report or Quick Stats database. Trends may shift with climate/drought pressures increasing irrigation demand.


determine the number of ponds and the size to decrease the amount of ground water to offset existing and planned hyperscaler usage

Thought for 53s
Ponds (on-farm surface water storage) can help reduce agricultural groundwater pumping for supplemental irrigation in central Ohio, thereby offsetting competition for aquifers from existing and planned hyperscalers (large data centers) and other industrial users. This aligns with concerns in the Central Ohio Regional Water Study (15-county region) about rising demands from climate-driven irrigation needs and data center growth.

Key Water Demand Context (Central Ohio Focus)

  • Agricultural irrigation (additional/future): ~9.15 billion gallons per year extra from groundwater across the 15-county region (projected for 2040+). This supports ~5 inches of supplemental irrigation per year in key areas (e.g., Licking County) during the critical growing season (roughly July–September). Peak seasonal demand reaches ~110 million gallons per day (MGD) during this period. Historically, most Ohio farming is dryland (no irrigation).
  • Hyperscalers/data centers (existing + planned): Concentrated in central Ohio (~95% of state capacity; ~130 facilities noted). Current capacity ~1.9 GW; projected to grow to 10–15 GW by 2030.
    • Direct water consumption (mostly evaporative cooling; often via municipal supplies that may draw from surface/groundwater mixes): Base case ~28.8 MGD by 2030 in central Ohio (range: low ~5.1 MGD to high ~43.7 MGD). Annual equivalent ~10+ billion gallons.
    • Broader industrial demand (including data centers/tech) projected much higher long-term (e.g., overall industrial to ~250 MGD by 2050). Individual large facilities can use hundreds of thousands to millions of gallons per day.
Data centers and projected ag irrigation are on similar orders of magnitude annually and compete (directly or indirectly via shared aquifers/municipal systems) in a region with abundant but finite resources, especially during droughts. Most data center water comes via utilities (not direct raw groundwater/surface draws), but overall pressure on resources exists. Ponds enable surface water alternatives for ag, reducing groundwater drawdown and freeing aquifer capacity or easing municipal strain.
Ohio has ample overall water (surface flows dominant in central Ohio; strong aquifers in southwest), but localized/timing issues, infrastructure, and lack of strict extraction rules create risks. Ponds help by capturing/storing runoff/rainfall for on-farm use.

Pond Requirements to Offset Groundwater Use

Ponds would supply (or buffer) the additional ag irrigation demand from surface sources (rainfall, runoff, streams—subject to permits) instead of groundwater. This directly decreases groundwater pumping by the offset volume.
Total storage volume needed (rough estimate for the 15-county region):
  • Core volume: ~9.15 billion gallons/year additional (or ~10+ billion gallons for peak growing season demand).
  • Add 20–40%+ buffer for evaporation, seepage, and dry-period reliability (common in pond design; losses vary by lining, depth, climate).
  • Total pond storage capacity required: Roughly 28,000–40,000 acre-feet (1 acre-foot ≈ 325,851 gallons).
    • Base: ~28,080 acre-feet for 9.15 BG.
    • With buffer: Scale up accordingly (e.g., ~36,500 acre-feet at +30%).
This is distributed storage across farms—not one giant reservoir. Ponds refill naturally via watershed runoff during non-irrigation or wet periods, so annual throughput can exceed static storage capacity. Design accounts for local soils, rainfall (~3 inches/month average, but variable), crop needs, and irrigation efficiency (e.g., drip vs. overhead).
Typical pond sizes (from NRCS and ag extension guidance):
  • Surface area: 0.25–several acres (popular <2 acres for manageability; larger for irrigation).
  • Depth: Minimum 6–8+ feet (deeper in center for fish/winterkill prevention; effective storage depth often 6–12 feet).
  • Capacity examples: 5–20+ acre-feet common for farm irrigation/livestock (supports supplemental irrigation on tens of acres of high-value crops; e.g., multiple inches over 20–50 acres). Larger shared or community ponds possible.
  • Example dimensions (approximate for ~10 acre-feet pond): Surface ~1–3+ acres, average depth ~8–10 feet, with side slopes (e.g., 2:1 or 3:1).
Number of ponds (illustrative scenarios, assuming even distribution and refill):
  • Smaller ponds (~5 acre-feet each, typical for individual farms): ~5,600–8,000 ponds region-wide.
  • Medium ponds (~10 acre-feet each): ~2,800–4,000 ponds.
  • Larger ponds (~20–50 acre-feet each, for bigger operations or shared use): ~700–2,000 ponds (or fewer if combined with other storage).
These would be implemented gradually as farms adopt irrigation (currently limited; hundreds of irrigated farms statewide). Not every farm needs one—focus on those shifting to irrigation or in high-demand areas. Many existing farm ponds could be expanded or optimized. Multi-use benefits: irrigation + livestock + wildlife habitat + recreation + stormwater management.
Practical considerations and caveats:
  • Site-specific design: Depends on watershed size (larger drainage area = better refill), soil (clay liners reduce seepage), evaporation rates, and crop/soil needs. NRCS provides planning tools; permits often required for larger withdrawals or modifications.
  • Costs and feasibility: Excavation/embankment ponds are cost-effective in suitable terrain. Lining adds expense but improves efficiency. Incentives or cost-sharing (e.g., via conservation programs) could help.
  • Limitations: Ponds excel for supplemental/buffer use, not replacing all needs in extreme drought without refill. Filtration/treatment may be needed for some irrigation systems. Surface water use has its own regulations/quality considerations.
  • Broader offset: This primarily targets ag groundwater reduction (~25 MGD average annual equivalent). It indirectly benefits hyperscalers by easing aquifer pressure and competition. Direct pond water for data centers is impractical (needs treatment; most use municipal). Efficiency improvements (better cooling tech for data centers, precision irrigation for farms) amplify benefits.
  • Regional scale: Focus on central Ohio (Columbus corridor) where both demands overlap most. Southwest Ohio (more groundwater-reliant) has different dynamics.
  • Uncertainties: Exact adoption rates of irrigation, climate variability, data center cooling tech shifts (WUE improving), and precise sourcing (many data centers use treated municipal water).
This is a high-level engineering estimate based on public studies—actual implementation requires hydrologic modeling, site assessments, and stakeholder coordination (farmers, utilities, regulators like Ohio EPA/ODNR). Larger-scale solutions could include expanded reservoirs, water reuse, or conjunctive use (surface + ground). Ohio appears positioned with sufficient resources overall through 2030+, but proactive planning (including storage) mitigates risks.
For precise local calculations, consult NRCS, Ohio State University Extension, or the full Central Ohio Regional Water Study reports. Visual examples of farm ponds (earthen embankments with vegetation buffers) are common in Ohio ag landscapes and support these multi-purpose goals.

Farm ponds for agricultural irrigation storage are vastly cheaper to build than data center cooling infrastructure (cooling towers/stacks and associated mechanical systems), often by orders of magnitude on a per-unit-water or regional-offset basis. This makes them an attractive, low-cost nature-based complement (or partial offset) to reduce groundwater competition from hyperscalers/data centers in places like central Ohio.

Pond Construction Costs (Farm/Irrigation Context)

From Ohio-specific and general U.S. agricultural/engineering sources:
  • Per acre-foot of storage: Roughly $1,000–$3,000 for basic pond construction (main cost driver for irrigation/drainage water recycling ponds; includes excavation and basic embankment).
  • Typical small-to-medium farm pond (e.g., 0.25–5+ acres surface, 6–12+ ft deep, earthen): $3,000–$23,000 average total, or $3,000–$15,000 per acre (lower for larger ponds).
  • Excavation alone: Often $300–$4,000+ per project or ~$3–$5+ per cubic yard, varying by soil, site access, and location (Ohio examples align with regional averages).
For the regional scale discussed previously (central Ohio 15-county area, ~28,000–40,000 acre-feet total storage to offset ~9.15 billion gallons/year additional ag irrigation demand, plus buffer for losses/evaporation/seepage):
  • Total estimated capex: Roughly $28–120 million (using $1k–$3k/acre-foot).
    • Lower end for simple earthen ponds with good sites/watersheds.
    • Higher end with liners, engineered spillways, permitting, pumps, or conveyance infrastructure.
  • Add 10–50%+ for design, permits, site prep, and distribution systems.
  • Per pond: A typical 5–10 acre-foot pond might cost $5,000–$50,000+ (or more for larger engineered ones). For thousands of distributed ponds, costs scale linearly but can be phased across farms adopting irrigation.
These are one-time capital investments with long lifespans (decades with maintenance), low ongoing opex (mainly occasional dredging or vegetation management), and multiple benefits (irrigation reliability, livestock/wildlife habitat, stormwater management, recreation). Costs vary widely by site-specific factors (soil type, depth, liner needs, regulations). Ohio ag contexts often cite these as feasible with potential cost-sharing via conservation programs.

Data Center Cooling Stacks/Towers Capex

" Cooling stacks" typically refer to evaporative cooling towers (or stacks/towers) used in water-based cooling systems for data centers/hyperscalers. These consume water via evaporation but are part of broader mechanical/cooling systems.
Key benchmarks (hyperscale/AI-optimized facilities):
  • Cooling towers specifically: ~$90,000–$100,000 per MW IT load (or ~$0.1M/MW). Example: ~$5.97M for a reference 70 MW hyperscale cooling tower system.
  • Broader cooling & mechanical (CRAHs/CRACs, chillers, evaporative towers, pumps, piping, plus rising liquid cooling components): $1.8–4.5M per MW (often 15–25% of total facility capex).
  • Total data center capex: Typically $11–20M+ per MW IT load (shell-and-core to full AI-optimized builds), with electrical/power infrastructure as the largest share (~40%+), followed by cooling/mechanical.
For context on scale (central Ohio projections):
  • Current ~1.9 GW capacity; growth to 10–15 GW by 2030 implies massive additional buildout.
  • Cooling-related capex alone for GW-scale additions would be in the billions of USD (e.g., towers/systems contributing hundreds of millions to low billions depending on water vs. air/hybrid/liquid designs).
  • Full facility costs dwarf pond networks: A single large hyperscale campus (100+ MW) can involve tens to hundreds of millions in cooling infrastructure.
Cooling towers are a relatively small slice of the overall cooling budget but are water-intensive (hence the relevance to ponds as an offset strategy). Shifts to air cooling, direct-to-chip liquid cooling, or immersion can reduce water use (and sometimes tower needs) but may increase other capex or energy opex.

Direct Comparison

  • Scale: Regional ag pond network (to meaningfully offset ~25 MGD average annual or peak seasonal groundwater demand) → tens to low hundreds of millions USD total. Distributed across farms → manageable per-project costs.
  • Data center cooling (towers + systems) → millions per MW; scales to billions for projected hyperscaler growth in the region. One large facility's cooling infrastructure can exceed the entire regional pond estimate.
  • Per unit water managed/stored: Ponds are far more cost-effective for storage and supply buffering (~$1–3 per gallon equivalent in bulk earthen construction). Data center water use involves continuous procurement, treatment, and evaporation losses plus high associated infrastructure capex.
  • Nature of investment: Ponds = relatively simple, nature-based, multi-benefit capital project with low opex. Cooling stacks/towers = integral, high-reliability engineered systems within much larger facility capex (total data center builds often $10M+/MW overall).
  • Trade-offs and context: Ponds help reduce ag groundwater pumping (freeing resources or easing competition), with co-benefits for farms and ecosystems. They don't directly supply data centers (which need treated water) but mitigate systemic pressure. Data center cooling capex is unavoidable for water-based systems but can be optimized via tech choices. Ponds require suitable land/sites and permits; data center systems require integration with power, redundancy (N+1 etc.), and ongoing energy/water costs.
Bottom line: Building the ponds to support ag irrigation offsets would be dramatically cheaper (likely 10x–100x+ lower total cost for comparable water impact) than the cooling infrastructure capex in data center builds. It's a cost-effective complementary strategy for water resilience in Ohio, though implementation would be gradual, site-specific, and farmer-driven. Full data center projects involve far larger overall investments where cooling is just one (important) component.
Costs are approximate/2020s-era and vary by location, design, inflation, and specifics—real projects need engineering quotes, soil tests, and permitting.
